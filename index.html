How does this work?

This tool calculates how many streams an artist needs to earn enough money to buy everyday items, based on the estimated per-stream royalty rate for each platform.

The formula is simple: Plays needed = Item price ÷ Per-stream rate. The result is rounded up to the nearest whole play.

Important caveat: These are estimates. Actual payouts vary enormously based on listener country, subscription tier, label agreements, distributor fees, and many other factors. An artist signed to a major label typically keeps only a fraction of even these amounts.

Royalties are also split between rights holders — the sound recording owner (usually label or distributor) and the songwriter/publisher. An independent artist keeping all rights receives the most; a signed artist might receive 15–25% of the displayed rate.

What can artists do?

Streaming is not a livable income for most independent artists. But there are strategies that help:

  • Stay independent. Distributing via DistroKid, TuneCore, or CD Baby means keeping a much larger share of royalties vs. signing to a label.
  • Merchandise & direct sales. A $25 t-shirt earns the equivalent of ~6,250 Spotify streams. Selling direct to fans via Bandcamp cuts out the middlemen entirely.
  • Live performances. Even small venue shows can earn more in one night than months of streaming. Ticket sales remain the most reliable artist income.
  • Sync licensing. Getting a song placed in a TV show, film, or advertisement can pay hundreds or thousands of dollars — a one-time payment worth millions of streams.
  • Patreon / fan subscriptions. A few hundred dedicated fans paying $5–10/month can exceed what streaming pays entirely.
  • Prioritize Tidal & Apple Music. When possible, promote to listeners on higher-paying platforms. Tidal pays ~3× what Spotify does per stream.
  • Advocate for change. Organizations like the Artist Rights Alliance push for better streaming legislation and fairer royalty structures. Support them.

The uncomfortable truth is that streaming economics primarily benefit platforms and major labels, not the artists whose work makes it all possible. Knowing that is the first step.
